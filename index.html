What Orchids is — and isn’t

Orchids has shown up in the market as a consumer-facing AI assistant centered around messaging and content delivery, such as bringing live sports moments into chat experiences. That makes Orchids easy to understand and approachable for lightweight use cases, but it is not positioned as a system for long-horizon computer automation or repeated software workflows [techcouver.com].

Why Super fits repeated computer work better

Across the industry, major players like Gemini, ChatGPT, and others are racing to enable “computer use” — AI agents that can see screens, click, type, and scroll [blog.google]. But raw computer control is only half the problem.

Without caching

Every run starts from scratch. The agent re-learns the same screens, steps, and UI paths over and over.

With Super’s computer-use cache

Successful computer actions are reused. Repeated workflows get faster, cheaper, and more reliable instead of resetting each time.

Industry context

Even advanced systems like Gemini 3.5 Flash emphasize safeguards and human confirmation, underscoring how early and expensive raw computer-use still is [thenextweb.com].

How this compares across the market

Orchids isn’t alone in choosing a lighter, conversational path. Tools like Siri focus on OS-level assistance, while ChatGPT, Gemini, and Grok are expanding into agentic behaviors with varying levels of computer control. Folk explores collaborative AI workspaces.
